A college student's journey of learning about UI/UX design

Introduction

Background

I am a college junior studying computer science. I took a UI/UX in order to learn about different aspects of CS than those covered in the classes I have taken thus far. During the first week of class, we looked at the portfolios of professional UI/UX designers. One that particularly stood out to me was that of Jesse Prague. Although she is now a product designer at Meta, many of the pieces on her website were those that she had designed while a college student and were projects specificaly geared towards fellow college students. Inspired by this, as I implemented various projects this semester, I decided to focus on designing things geared towards college students and investigating issues that my peers and I face regularly.

Wireframing

Overview and General Process

The goal of this project was to continue building off the skills developed in personas and storyboarding relating to identifying user groups and issues with interfaces and experiment Balsamiq to design a LoFi prototype that would potentially solve one of the issues identified for said user group. As mentioned above, for this project was inspired by the long lines of dining halls and how my peers and I often spent much of our lunch breaks either going from dining hall to dining hall to find one with short enough lines or just waiting in line.

When designing this wireframe, I first created a series of sketches of potential designs, then talked through these designs with a partner, determining which aspects of each design to use in the final version in order to make the hypothetical app as easy to use and navigate as possible.


In this version, there is a drop down window for each dining hall that rates the wait time for each section of the dining hall as "short," "medium," or "long." It also includes an average rating for the dining hall as a whole. At the top of the app, there is a scale that goes from 0 to 40min, to give the user a sense of what "short," "medium," and "long" mean.


Version 2 is very similar to version 1, except, instead of rating the wait time of each station as "short," "medium," or "long," it actually gives the time of the line. It also includes a feature at the top where users can log how long they waited in a given line (which would be factored into the calculations of the wait time).


Version 3 is very different from the other 2 versions. Rather than having a drop down tab for each dining hall, the user can instead click on a tab for a given dining hall to see the wait times (in minutes) for all of the lines in that dining hall. Each tab also contains a link to the menu for that specific dining hall, as well as a button that users can press to log a wait time.

Following peer feedback, I combined aspects of the different versions to make one "final wireframe." The final wireframe is most similar to version 2 in that it has drop down tabs for each dining hall with the wait time for each station displayed in minutes. It also contains the "log wait time" button at the top of the app.

Development

Overview and General Process

Finally, I got to move beyond prototyping to designing an actual webApp. For this project I was inspired by how my friends and I choose recipes to bake to design a WebApp that allows users to sort and filter recipes based on cooktime and dietary restrictions.

When developing this WebApp, I experimented with and learned about using separate components and state to allow users to trigger changes in the page. Specifically, users can click on the different radio buttons that determine how the data is filtered and ordered in order to change the state of the constants that determine these properties. Additionally, users can add/remove items to/from the "Favorites" list. When using state and components, I also learned about passing data through the different components via "props."
